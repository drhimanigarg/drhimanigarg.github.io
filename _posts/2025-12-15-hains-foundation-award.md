I am deeply honoured to be this year's LTH scholarship recipient of the Jan Hain Foundation for Scientific and Technical Research in the Environment and Climate — one of the biggest recognitions of my career so far. The award of 1.75 MSEK supports my project *Hydrogen for Climate-Neutral Fuels: A Strategic Initiative for Sweden's Energy Transition*, in which we use pore-scale and channel-scale simulations to improve water and gas management in hydrogen energy devices such as electrolyzers and fuel cells.

![Presenting the project at the Hains award ceremony at the Grand Hotel in Lund](/assets/img/hains2025-lecture.jpg)
*Presenting "Hydrogen for Climate-Neutral Fuels" at the award ceremony. Photo: Jessika Sellergren*

This year, 17 researchers from Lund University — working on everything from tick-borne diseases and dementia to climate change in the oceans — shared 13.5 MSEK from Hain's four foundations, and the award ceremony at the Grand Hotel in Lund on 15 December brought us all together for an afternoon of popular science talks. The audience included fellow researchers, deans, the university's Vice-Chancellor Erik Renström, and members of the public. Annika Olsson, Dean of LTH, said this year's LTH recipient will play a <a href="https://www.lth.se/article/from-ticks-to-fossil-free-researchers-awarded-for-their-work-himani-garg-at-lth-one-of-them/?L=0">"significant role in bringing us closer to a fossil-free and sustainable society"</a> — words that mean a great deal to me.

In my talk, I tried to make two-phase flow physics tangible for a broad audience — it turns out a glass of champagne is an excellent entry point, since champagne bubbles behave much like the hydrogen and oxygen bubbles inside an electrolyzer.
